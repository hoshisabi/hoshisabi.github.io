# Session Summary — April 25, 2026
*Icewind Dale · Session 1 · DDAL10*

---

## Executive Summary

The caravan was already kindling by the time Berg put the last wolf in the ground — and what came after wasn't a victory lap so much as an audition for a harder kind of courage. Four travelers who didn't quite know each other yet found out, over the course of one brutal night, exactly how much they were willing to haul each other through: ransacking wreckage for anything warm, reading a dead man's mail by firelight, outrunning a sky that had learned to cut, and standing their ground against owls the size of nightmares with spellbooks they had no business carrying. When the cave offered warmth and the underground stream asked nothing in return, it almost felt like the Dale was being generous — and then morning came, and the geometry in the snow made clear that something out there wasn't done with them yet. Pasha looked at the remains of their companion, said the hardest thing a guide can say, and led them into the white. They followed her. That was the last time any of them saw her. This session's emotional truth lives at the bottom of a mother's cookie tin, addressed to a son who'd stopped being able to read it: some things arrive too late, and Icewind Dale has no return address.

## Chapter by Chapter

**The Last Wolf**

The snow got what it came for first. The wolves had swept the caravan off the road like crumbs off a table — wagons overturned, the dead already cooling in the drifts — but there was one animal with enough nerve left to try and bolt. Berg let it get exactly far enough to think it had made a decision, then reminded it, politely and finally, that it hadn't. Nine damage. Done. The rest of the caravan was gone: swept away, buried, or both. What remained was the party, the cold, the dark, and their guide — Pasha — who looked at the sky the way experienced people look at things that have already decided to kill you, and said: *make camp, now, before this gets worse.*

**The Work of Getting Through the Night**

Nobody sat down. Berg moved through the wreckage with purpose, reading the terrain for somewhere defensible against both cold and avalanche, eventually finding an overhang that might shrug the worst of it sideways — not safe, but better. Dr. Medicine swept the debris with a sharper eye than his patent-medicine pitch would suggest and spotted brass fittings buried deep: a lockbox, dented but intact. Alina dug into the physical work, carving out what could be salvaged. Together they found enough timber for a fire and enough shelter to lower the odds.

Then came the crates. The wooden one gave up its secrets honestly — Berg's crowbar settled the argument on the first swing — and what was inside landed on the table with unexpected weight. A pink knitted cap and mittens, sized for an adult. A package of chocolate chip cookies, not yet stale. And a letter, handwritten, that nobody in the party had been meant to read: *Jordan, I heard things are getting worse in Icewind Dale. I'm so worried about you. I know you're a big adventurer now, but I hope these will help if you decide to stay. I love and miss you, Mom.* Jordan was somewhere under the snow. The warmth in the package had arrived with perfect timing for people who needed it and weeks too late for the one it was meant for. Dr. Medicine claimed the hat and mittens with the brisk efficiency of a man who knows what frost does to exposed ears, and when the Constitution saves came around, he rolled with advantage — and made it. Some of the others paid the tax that the Dale levies on the underdressed: frostbite, exhaustion, the first of what would be several footnotes to a very long cold.

The lockbox was more complicated. Berg took a crack with Sleight of Hand and hit a dirty 20 — it opened. Alina, whose fingers are usually eloquent, found herself unable to locate the keyhole, which is the kind of failure that gets told at every campfire for the next six months and bothers nobody except possibly Alina. What was inside: a velvet pouch, now soaked through, its contents — several potion vials — shattered and mixed into an improbable rainbow. One vial had survived the ruin intact. Alina touched the stained coins, and the colors decided to relocate to her skin. (It's relatively harmless, the DM said, with the specific energy of someone reassuring you about a thing that is only *relatively* harmless.) The intact vial: a Potion of Growth, waiting for a moment that deserves it.

Dr. Medicine took stock of his inventory, found a spare vial, added red food dye, and handed Berg a "speed potion." Berg held it up, considered that it was red, and chose to believe. This is, historically, how the best cons work.

**Quicksnow and the Sky with a Grievance**

Lightning showed on the horizon — blizzard incoming — and Pasha pointed at a mountain peak in the distance and said that was the direction of better shelter. They moved. She earned her keep on the way: she read the snowfield ahead and called out quicksnow — snow forming over water, identical to the safe kind until you step through it and find the cold water underneath that freezes you wet and kills you slower. With her guidance the party crossed it with advantage, and everyone made the check. One of the sidekicks went in anyway, pulled out sputtering, and the lesson was learned by proxy.

Then the wind stopped. Pasha said: *that's not good.* Before anyone could ask what specifically, the snow they'd just been navigating gathered itself into sheets with edges and came for them. "We're rolling initiative against the snow." Three rounds from cover. Everyone ran. Berg burned Adrenaline Rush to go faster. The razor snow took two slashing passes — one connected for 2 points of damage on someone who, in fairness, got extremely unlucky on both their attack roll against themselves and their failure to dodge — before the party made it into shelter.

The calm that followed was the wrong kind of calm. The snow moved in patterns. Yellow eyes opened in the white, not animal, not natural. Berg rolled Arcana and read the signature clearly: elemental manifestations, conjured, aimed. Something with a will had built these and pointed them here. Then, from above, came an owl hoot that landed on the nervous system like a bell struck inside a church that shouldn't be open at this hour. Wisdom saves. Alina felt the fear go all the way down to her bones.

**Four Owls with Spell Lists**

The owls were immense and wrong. Giant owls with Flyby, which is the game's way of saying they could tear through you and vanish back into the dark before you could make them regret it. Their spell lists — Detect Good and Evil, Detect Magic, Clairvoyance — were the choices of spies, not killers, which made the gap between what they were supposed to be and what they were doing feel deliberate and cold. Four of them came down.

River was first. A crit found the gap in her defenses for 11 damage, and then she was down in the snow with a death save failure on the first roll and the odds starting to work against her. Dr. Medicine made the professional's move: closed the distance fast, announced himself as a licensed chiropractor — this is not a credential that exists, but it was delivered with conviction — and deployed the Healer feat with the specific competence of someone who may be a fraud in most respects but is *not* a fraud about keeping people breathing. River came back to the fight. The dice had done their worst and it hadn't been enough.

Elsewhere on the field, things were nearly worse. The owls found Berg and Dr. Medicine in sequence: Berg caught two talons for 13 incoming damage, and used Interception as a shield between himself and the worst of it, eating the hit so his ally didn't have to. Dr. Medicine survived at 1 HP, which is the number that sounds like math and feels like a cliff edge. Alina kept the pressure up — arrows finding purchase shot by shot — and her Healing Word arrived for Dr. Medicine at exactly the moment it needed to, a spell tossed across the chaos like a rope to someone in cold water.

Creepy went down. One of the other NPCs took 18 damage in a single round from two owls coordinating, which is the kind of arithmetic that doesn't leave much room to argue with.

And then the table turned. The party's sidekicks, who had earned the right to be more than background characters, got their rounds. Pasha's longbow hit. The spellcaster landed a nat 20 and a 14 on their Nature cantrip — 6 and 7 damage — dropping one owl and badly winging another. Dr. Medicine's expert sidekick hurled daggers with the focus of someone who had a point to make: 3 damage, then 5, and the last owl came apart in a cloud of displaced snow. The blizzard had fielded its infantry and been answered.

**What the Cold Left Behind**

In the aftermath, the party found one more traveler: frozen solid in the act of raising a potion to her lips, hope arrested before it could become outcome. She was still wearing a platinum ring set with a large sapphire. They took the potion. They took the ring. The ring might mean something to someone. They'll know if they find out who she was waiting to become.

The group survival check to make it to shelter without another frostbite toll was close, but the numbers came up. The cave they found was the Dale being uncharacteristically decent: spacious, warm, sheltered from the wind, with a stream running cold and clean from a crack in the wall. They drank. They rested. Exhaustion dropped a level. Dr. Medicine started mentally cataloguing the spring water's branding potential while his companions slept.

**Geometry in the Snow**

Morning settled the account. Their companion — Dan, the bugbear who'd made it through the wolves and the razor snow and the elemental owls — was gone. What was left of him had been arranged. Not scattered: arranged. With the patience of something that thinks in shapes. The tracks said large creature, possibly bear-shaped. The arrangement said *message*. Pasha did not soften it. The thing had their scent now — blood memory, intention, an obsession that would show up every night until it got what it was owed. The only question was whether to wait for it to choose its moment or go find it while the blizzard hadn't quite finished arriving.

The party chose to hunt. That was the right call. Berg asked for togetherness — go, but go as one — which was also the right call, and which held for exactly as long as the snow let it. Pasha knew the trail and led them into the white. The blizzard did the thing blizzards do: it made corners into open clearings and trails into wrong turns, and somewhere in the middle of following a silhouette that turned out to be a wish, the party looked up and found an empty expanse that led, when they backtracked, to a cave that had no guide in it. Pasha didn't leave them. The snow left them for her. They returned. She didn't. The session ended on the sound of wind where an answer should have been.
